A STUDY ON TRANSLATOR BACKGROUND AND THEIR INFLUENCE ON TRANSLATION THEORY CHOICE

Minigame

Introduction

Eugene Nida is a pioneer in translation theory, especially in Bible translation. He introduced two key concepts: Formal Equivalence (FE) and Dynamic Equivalence (DE).

Research Background

Purpose of the study:

To examine how translator backgrounds affect the choice of translation theory.

Research Significance:

To understand the relationship between personal ideology, education, and translation strategies.

Nida’s Theory of Equivalence

Two Main Approaches

Formal Equivalence (FE)

Focus: Source text (ST).

Goal:

To preserve the original form and content as closely as possible, ensuring fidelity to the source.

Features:

  • Maintains original grammatical structure and vocabulary.
  • Retains cultural elements from the ST.
  • May sound unnatural in the target language (TL).

Use: Common in legal, academic, and religious texts.

Dynamic / Functional Equivalence (DE)

Focus: Target audience.

Goal:

Create an equivalent effect – TL readers should have a similar response as ST readers.

Features:

  • Prioritizes naturalness and clarity.
  • Adapts message to TL culture and context.
  • Emphasizes the principle of equivalent effect.

Use: Applied in literature, advertising, and Bible translation for outreach.

Nida’s Three-Step Translation Process

  1. Analysis: Analyze ST structure, meaning, and context.
  2. Transfer: Convert meaning mentally from ST to TL.
  3. Restructuring: Reconstruct message in a natural TL form.

Goals and Criticisms

Goals of Dynamic Equivalence

  • Natural and idiomatic expressions in TL.
  • Cultural and contextual consistency.
  • Readability and clear comprehension.

Criticisms of Nida’s Theory

  • "Equivalent effect" is subjective and hard to evaluate.
  • Loss of unique ST features, especially unique cultural or stylistic traits.
  • May oversimplify the translation process.

Meet the Translators

Huỳnh Sanh Thông

(July 15, 1926 – November 17, 2008)

Education and Career: Academic tone, footnotes, blank verse. Cultural preservation.

Key works: The Tale of Kiều, Lac – Viet Book Series.

Translation Features: Academic tone, footnotes, blank verse. Cultural preservation.

Theory Alignment: Julliance House’s Functional Equivalence.

Aileen Palmer

(April 6, 1915 – December 21, 1988)

Background: Political activism, interpreter during wars.

Key works: Prison Diaries (Hồ Chí Minh), poems by Tố Hữu.

Translation Features: Accessible language, Political clarity. Focus on reader understanding, less on form.

Theory Alignment: Functionalist (target-oriented, message-driven).

Case Study: Poem Translations

Ngắm Trăng (Moonlight)

Trong tù không rượu cũng không hoa,

A. Palmer: "For prisoners, there is no alcohol nor flowers,"

Her more generalized opening and use of "alcohol" show a focus on natural English phrasing and immediate resonance.

H.S. Thông: "In prison there's no alcohol nor flower,"

His translation is very close to the literal meaning, reflecting a commitment to lexical fidelity and directness.

Trước cảnh đẹp đêm nay biết làm thế nào?

A. Palmer: "But the night is so lovely, how can we celebrate it?"

She dramatically rephrases the line into a more poetic, emotionally charged question to inject a sense of longing.

H.S. Thông: "With what to celebrate this lovely night?"

This is a direct and accurate translation, maintaining the original's nuance with slightly more formal phrasing.

Người hướng ra trước song ngắm trăng sáng,

A. Palmer: "I go to the air-hole and stare up at the moon,"

Specifying "I" and using the evocative "air-hole" prioritizes personal image and emotional impact.

H.S. Thông: "I go to the window and watch the moon,"

Using "window" and "watch" is a straightforward, literal choice, preferring simplicity over interpretive imagery.

Từ ngoài khe cửa, trăng ngắm nhà thơ.

A. Palmer: "And through the air-hole the moon smiles at the poet."

"The moon smiles" is a highly emotional, anthropomorphic interpretation that creates a vivid and warm connection.

H.S. Thông: "The moon peers through the window at the poet."

"The moon peers" is a precise and less anthropomorphic verb, maintaining a more observational tone.

Đi Đường (On The Road)

Có đi đường mới biết đường đi khó,

A. Palmer: "Only when out on the road can we take stock of our dangers,"

She expands the meaning to "take stock of our dangers" for a more impactful and relatable message.

H.S. Thông: "He knows the road is rough who walks the road."

He maintains a close structure to the original with formal English phrasing, demonstrating formal equivalence.

Hết lớp núi này lại tiếp đến lớp núi khác;

A. Palmer: "After we climb one mountain, another looms into view:"

"Looms into view" is a dynamic and poetic verb choice that conveys the daunting challenge.

H.S. Thông: "One mountain range, another mountain range . . ."

The repetition and ellipsis maintain the cumulative feel of the original, mirroring its structure.

Khi đã vượt các lớp núi lên đến đỉnh cao chót,

A. Palmer: "But, once we have struggled up to the top of the mountain range,"

The use of "But" and "struggled up" emphasizes effort and enhances the emotional connection.

H.S. Thông: "Keep struggling up and reach the highest peak:"

"Keep struggling up" is a strong, accurate translation that maintains the sense of ongoing effort.

Thì muôn dặm nước non thu cả vào tầm mắt.

A. Palmer: "More than ten thousand li can be surveyed at a glance."

"Surveyed at a glance" powerfully conveys the visual scope and sense of achievement with natural English.

H.S. Thông: "ten thousand miles will come within your ken."

The use of the formal idiom "within your ken" highlights scholarly precision and lexical fidelity.

Limitations of the Study

  • Limited Scope: The analysis is based on only two poems and two translators. Findings may not be generalizable.
  • Qualitative Nature: Findings are interpretive and subjective, based on close reading, not statistics.
  • Complexity of Background: A translator's background is multifaceted and this study cannot account for all subtle influences.
  • Theoretical Focus: The analysis primarily uses Nida's theories; other frameworks could offer different perspectives.

Methodology & Implications

Theoretical Implications

  • Translator backgrounds are not neutral—they guide theoretical choices.
  • A scholarly background (Huỳnh Sanh Thông) leads to cultural preservation and overt translation.
  • A political-activist background (Aileen Palmer) supports message-oriented, functionalist strategies.
  • This suggests that translation is shaped by ideology, audience expectation, and personal values.

Aims and Objectives

The primary aim of this study is to demonstrate that a translator's background significantly influences their choice of translation theory and stylistic approach.

Specifically, the objectives are:

  • To compare and contrast translations by individuals with demonstrably different backgrounds.
  • To analyze the specific linguistic choices made in their translations to identify adherence to either Formal or Dynamic Equivalence.
  • To correlate these observed translational choices with the translators' personal histories, education, and professional specializations.
  • To highlight that translators are not neutral linguistic conduits, but active interpreters whose backgrounds shape their work.

Subject and Scope

This study focuses on two specific poems by Hồ Chí Minh, "Ngắm Trăng" (Watching the Moon / Moonlight) and "Đi Đường" (On the Road), and their English translations by two distinct translators: Aileen Palmer and Huỳnh Sanh Thông. The scope is limited to these four works to allow for a detailed, line-by-line qualitative analysis, providing concrete examples of theoretical application.

Methodology

Research Methodology: Qualitative analysis.

Main Activity: Close reading and detailed analysis of the original poems and their two English translations.

Focus of Analysis:

  • Translational choices at the lexical, grammatical, and stylistic levels.
  • These choices are evaluated in light of Nida’s theories of Formal and Dynamic Equivalence.

Additional Investigation: Examination of the biographical backgrounds of the two translators (Aileen Palmer and Huỳnh Sanh Thông), including their education, professional experience, and personal philosophies.

Final Step: Triangulating translational choices with the translators’ backgrounds to determine the influence of personal context on the application of translation theory.
